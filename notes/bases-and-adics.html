Bases & Adics

Last updated: Sep 11, 2025 draft
A friendly walk from ordinary number bases (binary, base-10, base-b) to the p-adic world: a different notion of “closeness” where numbers like 1, 10, 100, … crowd near zero — and repeating tails converge the “other” way.

1. What changes when a base changes?

Writing 349 in base-10 is secretly a dot product of place values: 3·10² + 4·10 + 9. In base-2 we swap 10 for 2, in base-7 we swap for 7, and so on. Nothing spooky yet — just different “rulers” laid across the same real number line.

Two levers matter:

  • Digits. Allowed remainders 0…(b-1).
  • Place values. Powers of b: … , b³, b², b, 1, b⁻¹, ….

2. Flip the telescope: the p-adic idea

Fix a prime p. Instead of measuring size with the usual absolute value, we measure how divisible a number is by p. The more factors of p, the smaller it is. This is encoded by the p-adic valuation v_p(n): the exponent of p in the prime factorization of n.

In the 5-adics, 125 = 5³ is “tiny” because it has three factors of 5. Meanwhile 3 is “large” (no factors of 5).

From this we define the p-adic absolute value: |n|_p = p^{-v_p(n)} (and |0|_p = 0). Distances follow the usual metric recipe d_p(x,y)=|x-y|_p. This metric is ultrametric: triangles are isosceles or better:

Strong triangle inequality: |x+y|_p ≤ max(|x|_p, |y|_p).

3. p-adic expansions look backwards

Real numbers converge when tails to the right stabilize (e.g., 0.999…). In p-adics, convergence happens when digits to the left stabilize in base p. So a typical 5-adic integer might look like … 0 3 4 1 (infinitely many digits extending left).

Formally, every p-adic integer has a unique expansion a₀ + a₁p + a₂p² + … with digits aᵢ ∈ {0,…,p−1}. Those infinite series converge in the p-adic metric because the terms p^k go to zero p-adically.

4. Constructing ℚp

Start with rational numbers and the distance d_p. Complete ℚ under that distance, just like completing ℚ under the usual metric gives ℝ. The result is ℚ_p, and its closed unit ball ℤ_p (numbers with |x|_p ≤ 1) behaves like the set of p-adic integers.

4.1 Units and divisibility

In ℤ_p, every number not divisible by p is a unit (it has a multiplicative inverse). Hensel’s lemma is the p-adic Newton method: simple root mod p ⇒ lifts to an actual root in ℤ_p.

5. Tiny examples

5.1 Geometric series “the wrong way”

In 5-adics, the geometric series with ratio 5 converges: 1 + 5 + 5² + … = 1/(1-5) = -1/4 in ℚ_5, because |5|_5 = 1/5 < 1.

5.2 “999…” phenomenon

The 3-adic number …222 equals -1 (in ℚ₃): it’s the limit of 2 + 2·3 + 2·3² + … which sums to 2/(1-3) = -1.

6. Reals vs. p-adics at a glance

  • Reals: size ≈ magnitude; p-adics: size ≈ divisibility by p.
  • Reals: decimals stabilize to the right; p-adics: base-p digits stabilize to the left.
  • Reals: usual triangle inequality; p-adics: strong (ultra) triangle inequality.

7. Where this goes

p-adics power elegant proofs (Hensel lifting), slick counting arguments (like LTE), and deep number theory (local-global principles). If you’re comfortable with base-b thinking, p-adics are that idea with a different notion of “closeness”.
